Who is Michael de Geus

Where instinct meets
execution

Michael de Geus is the rare kind of founder who doesn't wait for permission, funding, or perfect conditions. He identifies a problem, reverse-engineers a solution, and builds — often simultaneously across wildly different industries.

From corporate intelligence platforms and enterprise risk frameworks to tactical gear, performance beverages, marine exploration, and apparel — the throughline isn't industry, it's approach. Michael builds from first principles, with an inventor's obsession and an operator's discipline.

Author of The Corporate Security Playbook, creator of the Shadow platform and the HERO framework — he has quietly built one of the most eclectic and consequential portfolios of any entrepreneur working today.

A friend once described him as "James Bond, Indiana Jones, and MacGyver somehow merged into one person." Michael's only objection was that Bond always had a bigger budget.

The Origin Story

Every solution starts
with a moment

Michael didn't come from a family of entrepreneurs. He came from a conviction that most problems have elegant solutions — they just require someone willing to look at them differently and refuse to accept the answer everyone else settled for.

"I've never once started with the question 'can this be built?' I start with 'what would this need to be in order to actually work?' The rest is just engineering."

Corporate security was the first industry he decided to take apart and rebuild from scratch. What he found was an entire profession operating on inherited assumptions, legacy frameworks, and fear-driven budgets. He wrote the Playbook to replace them. Built Shadow to automate what the Playbook described. Founded HERO to operationalize it at scale.

And then — because Michael is constitutionally incapable of staying in one lane — he applied the same first-principles thinking to tactical gear, performance beverages, apparel, marine operations, and team performance. Different sectors. Same operating system.

The Builder's Method

How Michael
actually builds

The MacGyver part isn't a metaphor. Michael genuinely starts most builds with incomplete information, constrained resources, and a timeline that would make a reasonable person nervous. He finds this energizing.

007
The Bond Operating Mode

Strategic. Precise. Always three moves ahead. The polish isn't performance — it's preparation made visible.

IJ
The Indy Field Instinct

Some of the best decisions get made in motion, under pressure, with imperfect maps. The adventure is part of the process.

MG
The MacGyver First Principles

What do we actually have? What does this actually need to do? Build the answer from what's available, not from what's conventional.

01
Find the Real Problem

Most people solve the symptom. Michael spends an uncomfortable amount of time refusing to accept the obvious problem statement until he finds the actual one underneath it.

This is the part that looks like inaction. It isn't.

02
Discard the Inherited Playbook

Every industry has a conventional approach. Michael reads it carefully and then sets it aside — from the recognition that conventions calcify around old constraints that may no longer apply.

If everyone is doing it the same way, that's a signal, not a reason.

03
Build the Minimum Honest Version

Not the minimum viable product — the minimum honest one. The version that actually delivers on the core promise, even if nothing else is finished yet. Ship that. Learn. Iterate.

MacGyver didn't build a finished weapon. He built something that worked right now.

04
Run Toward the Hard Part

Every build has a moment where the hardest problem becomes unavoidable. Most founders delay that confrontation. Michael schedules it first.

Indiana Jones didn't avoid the boulder. He just ran faster.

05
Stay in the Field

The worst thing that can happen to a founder is losing contact with how their product actually performs in the real world. Michael never builds from behind glass.

Bond reports to M. He doesn't become M and stop going outside.

Core Philosophy

The Four Pillars of
Intelligent Security

01
Anticipate

Every crisis leaves footprints before it arrives. Michael's intelligence-first approach trains organizations to read the signal before the noise overwhelms them — and act on it before the window closes.

02
Architect

Security designed on first principles — not inherited frameworks. Every system, policy, and protocol built with intention and tested against reality. The MacGyver rule: use what works, not what's conventional.

03
Accelerate

Speed is a security advantage. Organizations that decide and act faster than their threats don't merely survive — they dictate the terms of engagement. Indiana Jones didn't outthink the boulder. He outran it.

04
Assure

True security is not the absence of threat. It is the confidence — earned through preparation, not assumed through optimism — that your people, processes, and posture are ready for whatever comes next.

Products have lifecycles. Knowledge compounds. Michael is in the business of building bodies of work — frameworks, methods, and institutions — that will still be defining their fields long after the next venture launches.
